The Deep Chart uses circles and rings to represent how the market is behaving in real time. They are not meant to tell you what to do, but to show what kind of behavior is currently taking place. Each symbol is a reading of market behavior, not an automatic signal.

Continuation Circles (Bullish / Bearish)

üîπ What they represent

Continuation circles indicate that the market is pushing decisively in the current direction, with coherence and follow-through.

  • Green ‚Üí bullish pressure
  • Red ‚Üí bearish pressure

The appearance of multiple circles in the same direction signals a progressive strengthening of the move.

üîπ How to use them

Read them only in the direction of the broader context. They support:

  • continuation trades
  • breakouts
  • pullbacks aligned with the main direction

Avoid using them against major reference levels

üëâ They do not trigger entries ‚Äî they confirm that the move is alive.

Absorption Circles (Defensive / Distributive)

üîπ What they represent

Absorption circles show that price is meeting active opposition:

  • The market attempts to move
  • A counterparty absorbs the pressure

Visually, they highlight a friction zone.

üîπ How to use them

Observe them near key levels. They indicate:

  • potential slowdown
  • possible rotation
  • preparation for an opposite move

They do not imply immediate reversal

üëâ These are awareness zones, not aggressive entry points.

Exhaustion Circles

üîπ What they represent

Exhaustion circles indicate that a move is losing efficiency after an extended push.

Price:

  • reaches an extreme
  • struggles to continue
  • becomes vulnerable to pullbacks or reversals

üîπ How to use them

Ideal for:

  • scaling out
  • taking profits
  • avoiding late chases

Always read them within the broader context

üëâ They are not automatic reversal signals, but warnings of declining momentum.

Silent Accumulation Circles

üîπ What they represent

Silent accumulation circles highlight phases where:

  • price moves very little
  • but underlying preparation is taking place
  • the market is quietly building direction

These are silent phases, often preceding visible expansions.

üîπ How to use them

Do not trade them directly. Use them to:

  • recognize buildup zones
  • avoid impulsive trades
  • prepare for future expansion

üëâ They signal active waiting, not action.

Rings

üîπ What they represent

Rings are the more sensitive and reactive version of circles. They express:

  • the quality of the move
  • the intensity of the reaction
  • the operational weight of the event

The size of the ring reflects how relevant that behavior is in the immediate context.

üîπ How to use them

Use them only around pre-defined levels. They help to:

  • refine timing
  • read acceptance or rejection
  • decide whether to act or remain neutral

Never use them in isolation

üëâ They are the final execution lens, not the starting point.

Optimizing Trade Exits

Contrary to what is commonly believed, the most decisive element in a trade is not the entry, but the exit. A good exit can turn an average entry into a profitable trade, while a poor exit can completely invalidate even the best entry.

Starting from this assumption, exits can be conceptualized and structured with the objective of optimizing profits and, at the same time, reducing risk.

Market behavior is not random. When price and time are observed over large intraday samples, their distribution tends to follow statistical regularities that resemble a Gaussian curve. Most price movements concentrate around the center of the distribution, while extreme extensions occur much less frequently and are structurally unstable.

For this reason, not every portion of a price movement should be traded. The goal is not to capture the entire move, but to consistently extract the statistically probable part of it.

Core Probability Zone

The central part of the distribution. This is where price moves most frequently, with the least resistance and the highest statistical reliability. This zone should always be considered the primary exit area and is where partial exits are statistically justified.

Extension Zone

This area represents a continuation of the move, but with a lower probability compared to the core. Reaching this zone requires structural coherence and orderly price flow. Exits in this area should be managed actively and never assumed as guaranteed.

Statistical Tail

The extreme part of the distribution. Movements in this zone are rare, unstable, and often followed by sharp pullbacks or reversals. While these extensions can occur, they should be treated as opportunistic exits rather than systematic targets.

Understanding Pullbacks

Every directional movement naturally includes retracements. Pullbacks are not signs of reversal, but rather a normal expression of market breathing. However, when pullbacks exceed their average statistical size, the probability of continuation decreases significantly. For this reason, pullbacks define natural boundaries for exits and risk control.

Exit proportions must also be adapted to the timeframe. On a 1-minute chart, movements develop quickly, ranges are smaller, and acceptable pullbacks are tighter. On a 5-minute chart, movements are wider, develop more slowly, and tolerate larger pullbacks. Exits must always scale with time to remain coherent.

The purpose of structured exits is not to maximize a single trade, but to standardize behavior, capture the core of the movement, and avoid exposure to unstable extremes. Consistency, not perfection, is the real objective.

Based on these principles, it is possible to extract average statistical proportions for 1-minute and 5-minute candles in terms of points and pullbacks. These values are not fixed targets and do not represent predictions. They are operational reference zones designed to support decision-making and risk management.
